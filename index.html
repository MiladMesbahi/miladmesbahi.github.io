Quasi-Octopus Robotic Arm Manipulator

January 2022 - March 2022

In our Solidworks CAD project, we aimed to design a robotic manipulator, inspired by the flexibility of an octopus tentacle and suitable for manufacturing. Our design adhered to seven key specifications, including achieving six degrees of freedom, emulating a ball joint's mobility, maximizing joint motion range, and ensuring simplicity in design and manufacturing with axial revolution, planar rotation, and Servo-Motor CAD. Our final design featured five subassemblies, each with unique components, utilizing a mix of off-the-shelf parts and 3D printing with ABS thermoplastic. Although our initial design met the basic criteria, future iterations would focus on joint complexity, weight optimization, and advanced manufacturing techniques.

Battle Bot

April 2022 - June 2022

In this project, we engineered a 1-pound combat robot, focusing on the optimal balance between durability and agility. Our design centered on a precise strength-to-weight ratio, utilizing high-quality aluminum to reduce weight while maintaining structural integrity. The robot's standout feature was an "egg beater" style weapon, strategically designed to undercut opponents effectively. This mechanism accounted for roughly 30% of the robot's total mass, aligning with our goal of maximizing impact while preserving maneuverability. Despite the robot being manually controlled via Bluetooth, considerable attention was given to the electronic setup and protection, ensuring functionality in intense combat scenarios. We leveraged Finite Element Method (FEM) analyses to identify and reinforce structural weak points, leading to iterative design improvements. Our compact and strategically designed robot achieved a notable second place in a highly competitive battle, showcasing the effectiveness of our engineering and design choices.

Autonomous Rover 2.0

January 2024 - June 2024

This rover took 2 quarters with 4 other students to build, fulfilling my senior capstone project at UCLA. The rover was designed to navigate an obstacle course, identify, collect, and deliver burger-forming discs using IR sensors and computer vision. As the mechanical design lead and co-lead for programming, I was in charge of designing the rover's robust structure and precise functionality while staying within a $500 budget. The drive system with Mecanum wheels provided omnidirectional movement, while a stepper motor-controlled claw handles stacking. The computer vision system, using a Raspberry Pi Zero 2 W and OpenCV, detected 4 die numbers on the discs to determine the collection order and drop off location. Our rover successfully met the requirements of the course, being one of the only groups to successfully complete the course and the only group to use camera software to detect the die numbers.

Research

NASA (JPL) - Mechanical Engineering Internship (Systems Testing)

June 2023 - September 2023

My summer long internship concentrated on various different tasks within the Dynamics and Environments Group at JPL. My main project was focused on evaluating chainmail solids (CMS) as a novel material for damping and shock mitigation in aerospace applications. We aimed to harness the unique properties of the CMS, such as low density and adaptable stiffness, for effective vibration management.

Our experimental procedure included a meticulous design of a Data Acquisition System and strategic placement of accelerometers to capture vibrational responses. The chainmail was subjected to random vibration profiles, and the results were analyzed using Power Spectral Density (PSD) comparisons and other modal analysis tools. Significant findings included a staggering -20dB reduction in transmissibility at critical lower frequencies and up to a -30dB reduction at higher, shock-level ranges, indicating the chainmail's effective damping capabilities. Finite Element Method (FEM) simulations complemented our experimental observations, affirming the potential of CMS in aerospace applications. The project's future focus will delve deeper into the chainmail's thermal properties and further tuning for specific aerospace applications, which we explored briefly through its implementation with cryocoolers.
